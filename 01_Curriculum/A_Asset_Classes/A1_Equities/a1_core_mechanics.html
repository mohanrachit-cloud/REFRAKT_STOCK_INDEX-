Part 01What Owning a Share Actually Means

1.1 The Bakery Analogy — Ownership in One Story

THE BAKERY ANALOGY: A bakery is worth ₹10,00,000 and is cut into 1,00,000 equal slices — each slice is a SHARE, priced at ₹10. Buy 100 slices for ₹1,000 and you own 0.1% of the bakery — every oven, every recipe, every rupee of future profit, proportionally. The bakery doesn't need to know your name or ask permission to keep baking. Your slice is a permanent, transferable claim on the business, not a loan to it. Own a bond and the bakery owes you money. Own a share and you ARE the bakery, fractionally, forever, until you sell your slice to someone else.

Equity is ownership. Not a promise, not a loan, not a coupon — a proportional legal claim on a company's assets and future profits. This is the single fact every other concept in this track builds on.

1.3 Primary Market — Where Shares Are Born

THE IPO PIPELINE: A private company wants public capital. It files a DRHP (Draft Red Herring Prospectus) with SEBI — a full disclosure document: financials, risk factors, how the money will be used. SEBI reviews, then the company opens a BOOK BUILDING window: a PRICE BAND (e.g. ₹95-₹100) is set, and investors bid within that band across 3-5 days. Retail category gets a reserved allocation (currently ₹2L max per retail application, UPI-based blocked-amount mechanism — money isn't debited until allotment). If oversubscribed, allotment is via lottery for retail. Shares LIST on NSE/BSE on a fixed date — first-day trading discovers the market price, which can open well above or below the issue price.

Application: Rachit applies for an IPO at the ₹100 upper price band, requests 100 shares (₹10,000 blocked via UPI mandate, not debited). If allotted, shares land in his demat account on listing day; if not, the block releases automatically — no manual refund chase needed like the pre-2016 system.

1.4 Secondary Market — Where Shares Trade After Listing

Once listed, shares change hands between investors on the exchange — the company itself is no longer party to the transaction. NSE and BSE are the two venues; NSE carries the deeper liquidity for nearly every large/mid-cap name.

THE ORDER BOOK — BID/ASK DEPTH: Every stock has a live order book: BUYERS stacked on the BID side (highest price first), SELLERS stacked on the ASK/OFFER side (lowest price first). The gap between best bid and best ask = the SPREAD. Tight spread (₹0.05 on a ₹500 stock) = high liquidity, easy entry/exit. Wide spread = illiquid, your market order can "walk the book" and fill at a worse average price than you expected. Kite's 5-depth window shows the top 5 price levels on each side — always glance at it before firing a market order on anything outside the top 100 stocks by volume.

1.5 Settlement — T+1 and What Actually Happens After You Click Buy

India moved to T+1 rolling settlement (trade date plus 1 working day) in 2023 — among the fastest settlement cycles globally.

DayWhat Happens
T (Trade Day)You buy/sell. Trade is executed and recorded by the exchange.
T+1Pay-in (buyer's funds, seller's shares) and pay-out (shares credited to buyer's demat, funds credited to seller's bank) settle via the clearing corporation.

Your demat account (via a Depository Participant — Zerodha routes through CDSL) is the electronic locker holding your actual share certificates in dematerialised form. No paper certificates change hands; the transfer is a ledger entry between two demat accounts, cleared through the exchange's clearing corporation as an intermediary guarantor — neither party can default on the other mid-trade.

1.6 Circuit Limits — The Exchange's Seatbelt

CIRCUIT BREAKERS: Every stock has a daily price band — typically 5%, 10%, or 20% above/below the previous close, set by the exchange based on the stock's volatility profile. Hit the UPPER CIRCUIT and no more buy orders can execute above that price — the stock is frozen at the ceiling until sellers appear or the next session. Hit the LOWER CIRCUIT and the mirror happens on the sell side. Separately, INDEX-WIDE circuit breakers exist for Nifty/Sensex moving 10%/15%/20% intraday — these HALT THE ENTIRE MARKET for a cooling-off period, a mechanism used exactly twice in the last decade (2020 COVID crash being the most recent). A stock hitting its circuit doesn't mean you're safe from further loss — it means you may be LOCKED IN, unable to exit, while the underlying business news keeps getting worse.
