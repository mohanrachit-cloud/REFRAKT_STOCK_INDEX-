Bucket 01Valuation Ratios — Is the Stock Cheap or Expensive?
The Valuation Trap: P/E of 10 is not automatically cheap. P/E of 40 is not automatically expensive. Compare always vs sector median, historical average, and growth rate (PEG). Context is everything.
METRIC 01 of 15
P/E Ratio — Price to Earnings
P/E = Market Price per Share ÷ EPS
NSE Large Cap: 20–25x fairDanger: >60x without growth

How many rupees you're paying for every ₹1 of profit the company earns. P/E of 25 = you're paying ₹25 for ₹1 of annual earnings. The market's price tag on profitability.

Buying a chai stall earning ₹1L/year. Someone asks ₹25L for it — that's P/E 25. Fair? Depends entirely on whether earnings are growing or shrinking.

Infosys: P/E 22–28x (steady IT). Zomato: 300x+ when loss-making — pure hope premium. Nifty 50 historical average: ~20x. Sector context is non-negotiable.

P/E much higher than sector peers without proportional growth = priced for perfection. Any earnings miss triggers brutal corrections. Negative P/E = loss-making company.
METRIC 02 of 15
P/B Ratio — Price to Book Value
P/B = Market Price ÷ Book Value per Share
Below 1.5x = undervalued watchAbove 5x needs ROE justification

Book Value = Assets minus Liabilities = liquidation value today. P/B tells you the premium the market pays over what the company is worth if it shut down and sold everything.

You own a flat worth ₹50L (book value). Someone offers ₹1.5Cr (P/B = 3). They're betting on the location's future, not today's value. Same logic applies to stocks.

HDFC Bank P/B ~3.5x — premium justified by consistent 16%+ ROE. SBI at P/B ~1.2x — cheaper but lower quality. P/B <1 can mean value trap, not always a bargain.

P/B below 1 is NOT automatically a buy. High NPAs in PSU banks inflate book value artificially. Always pair P/B with asset quality checks.
METRIC 03 of 15
PEG Ratio — The Growth-Adjusted P/E
PEG = P/E ÷ Earnings Growth Rate (%)
PEG <1 = potentially undervaluedPEG 1–1.5 = fairly valuedPEG >2 = expensive for growth

P/E tells you price. PEG adds growth to the equation. P/E 30 with 30% growth = PEG 1 (fair). P/E 30 with 10% growth = PEG 3 (expensive). Same P/E, opposite conclusions.

Two auto-rickshaw drivers earning ₹500/day. One static. One learning app-based driving and doubling annually. Would you pay the same for both businesses? PEG captures this difference.

Midcap IT: P/E 35, earnings growing 40% = PEG 0.87 → potentially cheap. TCS: P/E 28, growing 8% = PEG 3.5 → expensive relative to growth despite lower absolute P/E.

Bucket 02Profitability — How Efficiently Does It Make Money?
METRIC 04 of 15
ROE — Return on Equity
ROE = Net Profit ÷ Shareholders' Equity × 100
Good: >15% consistentlyExceptional: >25%Poor: below 10%

For every ₹100 of shareholder money in the business, how many rupees of profit did it generate? ROE 20% = ₹20 profit on ₹100 equity. Measures management efficiency with your capital.

You invest ₹1L in a biryani shop earning ₹20k = ROE 20%. Your friend's shop earns ₹8k on ₹1L = ROE 8%. Same capital deployed, very different returns. Every investor picks the first.

Asian Paints: ROE consistently 25%+ over a decade. That's exceptional compounding of shareholder wealth. PSU banks with ROE 6–8% barely cover their cost of equity.

High ROE through excessive debt is a trap. Always check D/E alongside ROE. A company borrowing heavily to inflate ROE collapses when interest rates rise. Use DuPont: ROE = Net Margin × Asset Turnover × Leverage.
METRIC 05 of 15
ROCE — Return on Capital Employed
ROCE = EBIT ÷ Capital Employed × 100
Good: >15%, ideally >WACCROCE < WACC = destroying value

Includes BOTH debt and equity capital (unlike ROE which only covers equity). Capital Employed = Total Assets − Current Liabilities. Better for capital-intensive businesses: steel, cement, telecom.

Factory using ₹50L own money + ₹50L bank loan = ₹1Cr capital employed. Earns ₹15L EBIT = ROCE 15%. This measures returns on ALL capital, not just your share of it.

Titan Company: ROCE ~35%+ — efficient, low asset intensity. Tata Steel: ROCE swings from 8% to 25% with commodity cycles. Cyclical companies always show volatile ROCE.

ROCE < cost of borrowing (WACC ~8–10% in India) = company destroying wealth with every rupee invested. No growth rate justifies this for sustained periods.
METRIC 06 of 15
Operating Profit Margin (EBITDA Margin)
OPM = EBITDA ÷ Revenue × 100
IT/Pharma: 20–35% healthyFMCG: 15–25% normalRetail: 3–8% typical

Of every ₹100 revenue, how much survives after operating costs but before interest and taxes. Measures core business efficiency without financing decisions distorting the picture.

Cloud kitchen earns ₹1L revenue, costs ₹70k running it — EBITDA margin 30%. Another earns ₹2L but costs ₹1.9L — only 5% margin. Bigger revenue, far worse business underneath.

HCL Technologies: ~25% EBITDA margin (asset-light IT). Reliance Retail: ~6% (volume-driven). Never compare margins across sectors — it's meaningless without context.

Margin compression over consecutive quarters even as revenue grows = rising input costs or pricing pressure. This precedes earnings downgrades. Watch closely into results season.
Bucket 03Business Quality — Can It Survive a Bad Year?
METRIC 07 of 15
Debt to Equity Ratio
D/E = Total Debt ÷ Shareholders' Equity
Ideal: <0.5 most sectorsTolerable: <1 capital-intensiveDanger: >2 non-infra

For every ₹1 of shareholder equity, how many rupees has the company borrowed? D/E 0.5 = ₹50 debt per ₹100 equity. High D/E amplifies profits in good times AND losses in bad times equally.

Flat bought for ₹1Cr — ₹30L own, ₹70L loan. D/E = 2.3. Property rises 20% = you made 67% on your capital. It falls 20% = you lost 67%. Debt is a lever — it cuts both ways.

Bajaj Finance: D/E ~5 — but it's an NBFC, lending IS its business. Nestle India: near-zero debt. Never compare D/E across sectors. Banks and NBFCs are structural exceptions.

Rising D/E + falling ROCE simultaneously = dangerous. The company borrows more but generates less return. This is how IL&FS, DHFL, and Jet Airways spiralled before collapse.
METRIC 08 of 15
Interest Coverage Ratio
ICR = EBIT ÷ Interest Expense
Safe: >3xWatch: 1.5–3xDistress: <1.5x

How many times can operating profit cover the interest bill? ICR 3 = earns 3x what it owes in interest. Measures survival capacity in a downturn — not just growth potential.

Salary ₹1L, EMIs ₹33k = ICR 3. Comfortable. Salary ₹1L, EMIs ₹90k = ICR 1.1. One missed bonus and you default. Companies work identically. One bad quarter and it's margin call time.

Reliance Industries ICR ~8–10x: fortress balance sheet. Vodafone Idea: ICR below 1 for extended periods — why it's been a debt trap destroying equity value relentlessly.

ICR declining consistently over 4–6 quarters even with revenue growth = interest costs eating the business alive. Often precedes credit rating downgrades and stock crashes.
METRIC 09 of 15
Promoter Holding & Pledging
BSE/NSE → Shareholding Pattern → Quarterly
Ideal: >50% held, 0% pledgedDanger: pledging >20% of holding

Promoters are founders/major owners. High holding = founders believe in the business. Pledging = promoter uses own shares as loan collateral. If stock falls, lender force-sells shares — crash accelerates. Cascading collapse.

Restaurant owner pledges his restaurant as collateral. Business dips, bank seizes the property. News of seizure scares customers away. Pledging = a doom loop waiting for the trigger event.

Zee Entertainment: heavy promoter pledging caused a 70%+ crash in 2019. DHFL: promoters pledged heavily before collapse. Bajaj group: near-zero pledging, decades of consistent compounding.

Promoter holding declining quarter-on-quarter is a silent exit signal. They know the business better than anyone. Consistent selling into price strength is an alarm bell — not a buying opportunity.
Bucket 04Growth Metrics — Is the Business Getting Better?
METRIC 10 of 15
Revenue & Earnings CAGR (3Y / 5Y)
CAGR = (End Value ÷ Start Value)^(1/n) − 1
Midcap target: >15% CAGRLarge cap: 10–15% respectableBelow 8% barely beats inflation

Compound Annual Growth Rate smooths year-to-year noise into a steady annual growth rate over multiple years. Always look at 3Y AND 5Y CAGR — single-year figures mislead badly and mask cyclical distortion.

Divide 72 by CAGR to know how long it takes to double. 15% CAGR = doubles in 4.8 years. 8% = doubles in 9 years. A company at 20% CAGR doubles its top line every 3.6 years. That is compounding power made visible.

Dixon Technologies: Revenue CAGR ~45% over 5 years (electronics manufacturing). HUL: ~8% CAGR — mature and stable but slow. Markets pay very different multiples for these two trajectories.

Revenue growing but earnings CAGR flat = margin erosion. The company grows the top line by sacrificing profitability — often a price war signal or a loss of competitive moat.
METRIC 11 of 15
Earnings Consistency — O'Neil's CAN SLIM Connection
Check: Quarterly EPS trend over 8–12 quarters
O'Neil: 25%+ EPS growth last 2 qtrsTwo consecutive misses = exit signal

Not just how much earnings grew — how consistently. One good year and three bad years is not a compounder. You want steady, ideally accelerating EPS growth across at least 8 quarters with no major misses.

Two students. Ravi: 90, 92, 89, 93, 95 — consistent. Priya: 95, 60, 88, 45, 92 — volatile. Who gets the scholarship (premium valuation)? Ravi. Institutions buy consistency, not occasional brilliance.

Bajaj Finance: consistent 25–35% EPS growth over multiple years — commanded 8–10x P/B premium as a result. Volatile quarterly results = lower PE multiples. Simple as that. Check on Screener.in 10Y view.

Bucket 05Cash Flow Health — The Fraud Detector
The Golden Rule of FA: A company can show profit on paper through accounting adjustments. It cannot fake cash in the bank. If Net Profit rises but Operating Cash Flow falls — someone is lying. Check this before every single investment.
METRIC 12 of 15
Operating Cash Flow vs Net Profit
CFO/PAT Ratio = Operating Cash Flow ÷ Net Profit
Healthy: CFO/PAT >1Acceptable: 0.7–1Danger: consistently <0.5

Net Profit includes accruals — revenue recorded but not yet received as cash. OCF shows actual cash generated from operations. A healthy business should have OCF ≥ Net Profit consistently across years.

Contractor shows ₹10L "profit" — but ₹8L is unpaid client invoices. Actual cash received = ₹2L. OCF = ₹2L, Net Profit = ₹10L. This is either a receivables problem or outright fraud. Both are bad.

Satyam: Net Profit rising, OCF suspicious for years before ₹7,000Cr fraud surfaced. PCJ (PC Jeweller): large receivables vs profit — stock crashed 80%. This check takes 2 minutes on Screener.in.

Receivables growing faster than revenue = customers not paying = channel stuffing or fake sales. India's most common accounting fraud pattern. Check receivables days trend over 3–5 years every time.
METRIC 13 of 15
Free Cash Flow — The Real Measure of Value
FCF = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditure
Positive FCF 5+ years = qualityNegative FCF OK in high-growth phaseNegative FCF in mature biz = trap

After the business generates cash from operations AND spends what it needs to maintain/grow assets (capex), what's left over? FCF = what can be returned to shareholders or reinvested at high ROCE.

Mango orchard earns ₹5L from fruit (OCF). Costs ₹1L to maintain the trees (capex). FCF = ₹4L. This ₹4L is the real value the orchard creates — yours to reinvest, distribute, or save.

HUL: asset-light FMCG, consistently strong FCF — steady dividends and buybacks flow from it. Vodafone Idea: massive negative FCF for years from spectrum capex. Burning cash constantly. Equity = zero.

METRIC 15 of 15
Tax-Adjusted Return — Your 30% Reality Check
Post-Tax Return = Gross Return × (1 − Tax Rate)
STCG <12 months: 20% taxLTCG >12 months: 12.5% above ₹1.25L

Your gross return is not your real return. STCG (<12 months) = 20% flat. LTCG (>12 months) = 12.5% on gains above ₹1.25L/year. Factor this into every hold/sell decision.

Quick mental math: divide any gain by 1.1 for rough LTCG post-tax estimate. ₹1L gain → ÷1.1 → ~₹91k real gain. For STCG: divide by 1.25. Fast approximation for on-the-spot decisions.

18% STCG return (10 months) → post-tax = 14.4%. 15% LTCG (13 months) → post-tax = 13.1%. The "lower return" stock nets more after tax. Holding period matters more than most investors realize.

At 30% income slab, short-term trading tax drag is enormous. A trader making 25% gross and paying 20% STCG earns 20% net. A long-term investor making 20% gross at 12.5% LTCG earns 17.5% net. Time in market beats timing the market — and the tax code proves it mathematically.

B10 · FINAL QUIZFA Fundamentals
Q1 of 6 · Valuation
A company has P/E of 45 and earnings growth of 45% YoY. Its PEG ratio is:
A 2.0 — expensive
B 1.0 — fairly valued
C 0.5 — undervalued
D Cannot be calculated
✅ PEG = 45 ÷ 45 = 1.0. Fairly valued. PEG <1 = bargain territory; >2 = expensive for the growth on offer. Same P/E can mean very different things depending on the growth rate behind it.
Q2 of 6 · Profitability
Company A: ROE 28%, D/E 0.2. Company B: ROE 30%, D/E 3.5. Which is the better quality business?
A Company B — higher ROE wins always
B Equal — both have strong ROE
C Company A — ROE without excessive debt is genuine
D Cannot judge without revenue data
✅ Company A. High ROE through massive leverage (D/E 3.5) is fragile — one downturn and interest costs devour profits. Company A earns strong returns on mostly equity capital = sustainable competitive moat.
Q3 of 6 · Fraud Detection
A company reports Net Profit growing 25% YoY but Operating Cash Flow has declined for 3 consecutive years. This most likely indicates:
A Normal — profits lead cash flows
B Great — profits growing fast
C A capex-heavy expansion phase
D Potential manipulation — receivables inflating profit
✅ D. Profit growing + OCF declining = receivables piling up (sales recorded but cash not received). Classic fraud setup seen in Satyam, PCJ, and dozens of Indian small-caps. Exit immediately or at minimum investigate receivables days trend over 3–5 years.
Q4 of 6 · India Layer
Promoter holding dropped from 68% to 61% over 4 consecutive quarters while stock price was rising. Most likely interpretation?
A Silent exit signal — selling into price strength
B Normal — SEBI mandates promoter dilution
C Bullish — lower promoter holding = more float
D Irrelevant — only pledging matters
✅ A. Promoters know the business better than anyone. Consistent selling across 4 quarters — especially into price strength — is a silent alarm. They may see growth plateauing or regulatory risks before the market does. Don't ignore this data point.
Q5 of 6 · Tax
You earn ₹80,000 profit from a stock sold after 10 months. Tax liability in India (post-July 2024)?
A Zero — below ₹1.25L threshold
B ₹16,000 — STCG at 20%
C ₹10,000 — LTCG at 12.5%
D ₹24,000 — 30% income tax applies
✅ B. Held <12 months = STCG. Tax = 20% flat. ₹80,000 × 20% = ₹16,000. The ₹1.25L exemption only applies to LTCG, not STCG. Holding period determines the tax bracket — check before every sell decision.
Q6 of 6 · Integration
FA complete — ROE 22%, D/E 0.3, OCF healthy, 18% CAGR. What is your NEXT step before buying?
A Buy immediately — FA is done
B Calculate DCF and compare to market price
C Open TradingView — find the TA entry trigger
D Wait for next quarterly results
✅ C. FA gave you conviction — you know WHAT to buy. TA gives you WHEN. A great company bought at a technically wrong point (overbought, at resistance, in downtrend) still causes short-term pain. Use RSI, support levels, and trend confirmation before entering. FA + TA = your actual edge.
