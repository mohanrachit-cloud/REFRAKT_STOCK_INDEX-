What is a Candlestick? — The Premise

Body Open to Close range. Wide = strong move
Upper Wick Highest price reached — rejected
Lower Wick Lowest price reached — rejected
Green/Bull Close ABOVE open — buyers won
Red/Bear Close BELOW open — sellers won

A candlestick is a single visual unit that captures four pieces of data about a trading period — the Open, High, Low, and Close — in one instant. Invented by Japanese rice traders in the 18th century, centuries before Western bar charts existed.

The body (the thick rectangle) shows the distance between opening and closing price. A green body means buyers pushed price up during the period — close is above open. A red body means sellers pushed price down — close is below open.

The wicks (thin lines above and below the body) show the extremes — how high or low price reached before being rejected. Long upper wicks mean bulls tried to push higher but were rejected by sellers. Long lower wicks mean bears tried to push lower but were rejected by buyers.

The key insight: Candlesticks reveal the battle between buyers and sellers in every single period. The size, shape, colour, and position of the body and wicks tells you who won, who lost, and how convincingly. Patterns across 1-5 candles reveal where the battle is turning.

Murphy's rule: Candlestick signals must always be confirmed by the next candle and by volume. A pattern in isolation is a suggestion. A pattern confirmed by the next candle with expanding volume is a signal.

↑ Bullish Bullish Reversal Patterns Appear at market BOTTOMS — signal bears losing control

⚠ Read This First — What "Bullish Reversal" Actually Means

Prior trend required: DOWNTREND. A bullish reversal pattern appears at the END of a falling market — a selling spree that is running out of steam. "Bullish" describes the OUTCOME not what came before. Before these patterns: bears were in control, price was falling. After these patterns: bulls take over and price begins rising.

Think of it as a tug of war. Bears have been pulling the rope (price) downward. They are getting exhausted. Bulls grab the rope and start pulling back. The moment price stops falling and starts turning up — that is the bullish reversal. You are identifying the exact candle(s) where the bears lost their grip.

Murphy's non-negotiable rule: Every pattern below is meaningless unless it appears after a clear prior downtrend. A Hammer in the middle of an uptrend is noise. The same Hammer at the bottom of a 3-week selloff at a key support level is one of the most powerful signals in technical analysis. Prior trend context is the first filter — always.

Hammer 1 candle
Isolation
Prior trend: DOWNTREND

Psychology: Bears drove price sharply lower during the session — but bulls fought back completely, closing near the high. The long lower wick is the tell: sellers tried and failed. Bulls are awakening at a price level where buyers see value.

📐 Key rule: Lower wick must be at least 2× the body length. Upper wick tiny or absent. Body at the upper end of the range. Colour (green/red) is secondary — the long lower wick is the signal.

Isolation
Prior trend: DOWNTREND

Psychology: Bulls attempted a rally — the long upper wick shows their effort. Bears pushed price back down but couldn't hold it. A tentative first attempt by bulls, needs next green candle to confirm the buyers are serious.

📐 Key rule: Upper wick must be at least 2× the body. Lower wick tiny or absent. Weaker signal than Hammer — needs a strong green confirming candle the next session to be valid.

Isolation
Prior trend: DOWNTREND

Psychology: Buyers dominated the entire session from open to close. Decisive bullish control with little hesitation. After a downtrend, signals a powerful shift in momentum. The wider the body, the stronger the signal.

📐 Key rule: Body should be significantly larger than recent candles — visually dominant. Small or no wicks. After a downtrend, the sheer size of the green body shows a decisive shift in power.

Isolation
Prior trend: DOWNTREND

Psychology: Opens at the low of the day with a gap down — bears appeared to be in total control. But bulls immediately took over and never looked back. No lower wick means bears had zero control after the open. Powerful reversal signal.

📐 Key rule: Opens at or near the low of the day with a gap down (no lower wick). Closes near the high. The absence of a lower wick is critical — it means bears had zero control after the open.
Engulfing Pattern (Bullish) 2 candles
Isolation
Prior trend: DOWNTREND

Psychology: A large green candle completely swallows the previous red candle's body. Bulls have overwhelmed bears in a single session with decisive force. The bigger the engulf, the more convincing the reversal. One of Murphy's most reliable 2-candle patterns.

📐 Key rule: The green candle's body must completely engulf (open below and close above) the prior red candle's body — wicks are not counted. The larger the engulf, the stronger. Ideally on above-average volume.

Prior trend: DOWNTREND

📐 Key rule: The second candle (green) must be entirely contained within the body of the first (large red). Think of it as the small candle fitting inside the large one. Less powerful than Engulfing — needs confirmation.

Isolation
Prior trend: DOWNTREND

Psychology: Same as Harami but second candle is a Doji — perfectly balanced indecision. Maximum stalemate — buyers and sellers exactly matched. After a downtrend this signals total exhaustion of selling pressure. Stronger than regular Harami.

📐 Key rule: The second candle must be a Doji (open = close, or nearly so) contained within the prior red body. The Doji shows perfect deadlock. Stronger than regular Harami precisely because of this absolute indecision.
Piercing Line 2 candles
Isolation
Prior trend: DOWNTREND

Psychology: Bulls gap down at open (appearing to confirm downtrend) then surge past the midpoint of the previous red candle. This reversal catches bears off guard. The 50% threshold is critical — it shows bulls have genuinely overpowered the prior session's sellers.

📐 Key rule: Second candle (green) must open BELOW the prior red candle's low (gap down) and close ABOVE the midpoint (50%) of the prior red body. The deeper it closes into the red body, the stronger the signal. Closing below 50% makes it an 'In Neck Line' — much weaker.

Psychology: After a downtrend, a Doji appears with a gap down — pure indecision at a new low. Bulls and bears in a deadlock at the extreme. The market doesn't know which way to go next. Typically resolved upward at support zones when sellers are truly exhausted.

📐 Key rule: The Doji must gap away from the prior red candle — ideally with no overlap of bodies. The gap isolation amplifies the signal. Needs a confirming green candle to complete the reversal.

Isolation
Prior trend: DOWNTREND

Psychology: Three consecutive long green candles, each opening within the previous body and closing progressively higher. Bulls are methodically taking control session by session — no panic, just sustained institutional buying. One of the most reliable bull reversal signals.

📐 Key rule: Each candle should open within the body of the prior candle (not gap up) and close near its high. Wicks should be small. Avoid if third candle shows a notably long upper wick — that indicates exhaustion and weakens the pattern.

📐 Key rule: Three-candle pattern. Candle 1: large red. Candle 2: small body (star) that gaps down from candle 1 — the gap is key. Candle 3: large green that closes above the midpoint of candle 1's body. The star should not overlap candle 1's body.

📐 Key rule: Same as Morning Star but candle 2 must be a Doji. The Doji makes this pattern MORE powerful — open equals close is absolute indecision, not just small body indecision. The gap between candle 1 and the Doji strengthens the signal.

Isolation
Prior trend: DOWNTREND

Psychology: The rarest and most powerful 3-candle reversal. The Doji gaps completely away from both surrounding candles — completely 'abandoned'. Gap-isolated indecision at the bottom followed by a gap-up green candle signals institutional capitulation followed by immediate reversal.

📐 Key rule: The Doji must gap away from BOTH surrounding candles with no wick overlap. This complete isolation — the Doji 'abandoned' by both neighbours — is what makes it the rarest and most powerful reversal. Extremely rare; treat it as a very high conviction signal when it appears.

📐 Key rule: Both candles must close at precisely the same price level. The green candle must open below the prior red close (gap down) then close exactly at the same level. The shared close price becomes a significant support level.

📐 Key rule: Both candles must be Marubozu — no wicks at all. A gap must exist between them. The gap + zero wicks on both candles = extreme directional conviction in both sessions. Any wicks weaken this pattern significantly.

⚠ Read This First — What "Bearish Reversal" Actually Means

Prior trend required: UPTREND. A bearish reversal pattern appears at the END of a rising market — a bull run that is running out of fuel. "Bearish" describes the OUTCOME not what came before. Before these patterns: bulls were in control, price was rising. After these patterns: bears take over and price begins falling.

The same tug of war — but now the bulls have been pulling for weeks and are exhausted. The bears grab the rope and start dragging price downward. These patterns identify the exact candle(s) where the bulls lost their grip at the top.

Murphy's non-negotiable rule: A Shooting Star at the top of a 4-week rally at a known resistance level is a high-conviction sell signal. The same candle shape appearing in the middle of a downtrend is meaningless. Always identify the prior uptrend first. Then look for the pattern.

📐 Key rule: Identical shape to the Hammer — long lower wick at least 2× the body, small or no upper wick. BUT it appears at the TOP of an uptrend, not the bottom. This is the most important context distinction in all candlestick analysis. The same candle, completely opposite meaning.

→ Bull Continuation Bullish Continuation Patterns Appear DURING uptrends — signal the trend resuming after a pause

⚠ Read This First — What "Bullish Continuation" Actually Means

Prior trend required: UPTREND already in progress. Bullish continuation patterns do NOT appear at bottoms. They appear in the MIDDLE of an existing uptrend — during a brief pause, pullback, or consolidation. The signal: the pause is ending and the uptrend is resuming. Bulls were in control, took a breath, and are now back.

Think of it like a sprinter who pauses to catch their breath mid-race — but then accelerates again. The pause looked like they were stopping, but they weren't. These patterns tell you the sprint is resuming. They are NOT reversal signals — they confirm the existing direction.

Murphy's rule: Continuation patterns require confirmation that price is resuming in the direction of the prior trend. The key question is always: "Is this pullback a pause or a reversal?" These patterns answer: it was a pause.

Rising Three Methods 5 candles
Isolation
Prior trend: UPTREND (continuing)

Psychology: A large green candle (the trend), then three small red candles that stay within the first candle's body — profit-taking pause contained within the trend. Then a new large green candle resumes the uptrend. The shallow pullback was healthy consolidation — bulls held the line.

📐 Key rule: Five candles. Candle 1: large green. Candles 2-4: small red candles that stay WITHIN candle 1's body range — they must not close below candle 1's open. Candle 5: large green that closes above candle 1's high. The containment of the pullback within candle 1 is essential.

Isolation

⚠ Read This First — What "Bearish Continuation" Actually Means

Prior trend required: DOWNTREND already in progress. Bearish continuation patterns appear in the MIDDLE of an existing downtrend — during a brief bounce or relief rally. The signal: the bounce is a trap and the downtrend is resuming. Bears were in control, allowed a small recovery, and are now pressing lower again.

Think of it like a stone dropped from height — it bounces once on hitting a surface, but then rolls downward again. That bounce is not a reversal. It is a continuation. These patterns identify that the apparent recovery is being absorbed by sellers who use it as a chance to reload short positions.

Murphy's rule: Do not confuse a continuation bounce with a genuine reversal. These patterns appear most powerfully when the bounce candles are contained within the prior large red candle's range — the bears never truly lost control.

⚡ Confluence Candlesticks + Indicators — The Real Edge Murphy's rule: candlesticks signal direction, indicators confirm conviction

A candlestick pattern alone is a suggestion. A candlestick pattern at a key support/resistance level, confirmed by RSI divergence and above-average volume — that is a high-conviction trade. Here are four examples of confluence in action.

Example 1 Hammer + RSI Oversold + Support Level
How to read this: Price is falling and reaches a known support level. A Hammer forms exactly at support — bulls rejected the selloff intraday. RSI simultaneously crosses below 30 (oversold) and begins turning up — divergence forming. Volume should be above average on the Hammer candle. Three-condition confirmation: pattern + support level + RSI signal = high conviction long entry. Stop loss just below the Hammer's low.
Example 2 Evening Star + RSI Overbought + Resistance
How to read this: Price rallies into a known resistance zone. An Evening Star forms right at resistance — the star shows exhaustion at the high, the red candle confirms sellers taking over. RSI was above 70 (overbought) and begins rolling over — bearish divergence forming. Pattern + resistance + RSI rollover = high conviction short or exit long. This is exactly the type of setup Murphy describes as the highest reliability combination.
Example 3 Bullish Engulfing + Volume Surge + 200 MA Bounce
How to read this: Price pulls back to the 200 MA — a major long-term support. A small red candle touches the MA, then a large Bullish Engulfing pattern forms — with volume at 2x the average. This triple confluence is one of the most reliable setups in Murphy's entire framework: key MA support + bullish reversal pattern + institutional volume confirmation. Three filters firing simultaneously. The Engulfing candle with outsized volume is the institutional footprint — smart money defending the MA.
Example 4 Morning Star + MACD Bullish Crossover
How to read this: A Morning Star pattern forms during a downtrend — the three-act reversal playing out on the price chart. Simultaneously, the MACD line (blue) crosses above the signal line (red dashed) — the momentum indicator confirming what the candlestick is showing. When a high-quality 3-candle reversal pattern coincides with a MACD crossover, you have two independent systems giving the same signal at the same time. Murphy's rule: convergence of signals from independent methods dramatically increases reliability.
Murphy's Golden Rules for Candlestick Trading
Rule 1 — Trend firstAlways identify the prior trend. A Hammer at the bottom of a downtrend is powerful. A Hammer in the middle of an uptrend is noise.
Rule 2 — ConfirmEvery candlestick signal requires confirmation from the next candle. Never act on a pattern before the confirming candle closes.
Rule 3 — VolumeA reversal pattern on below-average volume is unreliable. Volume above the 20-period average on the key candle is the institutional footprint.
Rule 4 — LocationContext multiplies power. A pattern at a key support/resistance, Fibonacci level, or moving average is 3-5x more reliable than the same pattern in empty space.
Rule 5 — ConfluenceCandlestick + RSI signal + MACD crossover + volume = three independent systems agreeing. This is where the real edge lives — not in the pattern alone.
Rule 6 — Stop lossEvery candlestick trade has a natural stop: just below the low of a bullish pattern, just above the high of a bearish pattern. The pattern itself defines the invalidation.
