Part 01What a Bond Actually Is

1.1 The IOU Analogy

THE IOU ANALOGY: Buying a share makes you an OWNER. Buying a bond makes you a LENDER. Lend the Government of India ₹1,00,000 via a 10-year G-Sec and it promises two things in writing: a fixed COUPON (interest) paid on schedule, and your ₹1,00,000 PRINCIPAL back at maturity. Unlike a share, a bond has an expiry date and a fixed, contractual repayment — it does not participate in the borrower's upside, and in exchange it sits senior to equity if the borrower ever goes bankrupt. Equity is a bet on growth; a bond is a bet that the borrower pays you back.

1.2 Coupon vs. Yield vs. YTM — Three Different Numbers

TermDefinitionExample
Coupon RateFixed % of FACE VALUE paid annually, set at issuance, never changes₹1,000 face value bond, 7% coupon = ₹70/year, always
Current YieldCoupon ÷ CURRENT MARKET PRICE (moves as price moves)Same bond trading at ₹950 → yield = 70/950 = 7.37%
YTM (Yield to Maturity)Total annualised return if held to maturity — coupon + price gain/loss to par, compoundedThe number that actually lets you compare two different bonds fairly

1.3 Why Bond Prices Move Opposite to Interest Rates

THE OLD SOFA ANALOGY: You bought a sofa (bond) that pays a fixed 7% "comfort yield" forever. A newer sofa model launches paying 9%. Nobody wants your old 7% sofa at full price anymore — its resale value drops until its EFFECTIVE yield (price-adjusted) also reads roughly 9%, so it stays competitive. That's exactly what happens when the RBI raises interest rates: newly issued bonds carry higher coupons, so existing lower-coupon bonds must fall in PRICE to offer a comparable yield. Rates up → bond prices down. Rates down → bond prices up. This inverse relationship is the single most important mechanical fact in fixed income.

2.1 Sovereign Gold Bonds — Worth Calling Out Specifically

SGBs get two return streams other bonds don't: a fixed 2.5% annual interest PLUS full exposure to gold price appreciation, paid in cash but denominated in grams of gold. Held to full 8-year maturity, capital gains are completely tax-exempt — a meaningfully better structure than buying physical gold (no making charges, no storage risk, no GST) or gold ETFs (which don't carry the tax exemption on maturity). The catch: an 8-year lock-in for the tax benefit, though early exit is possible on the exchange after 5 years with normal capital gains tax applying.

THE YIELD-CHASING TRAP: A bond paying 11% when AAA paper pays 7.5% isn't a free lunch — it's the market pricing in a real chance of default. Every high-profile Indian bond default of the last decade (IL&FS, DHFL) was preceded by yields that looked "too good" relative to AAA paper for months before the actual default. Extra yield in bonds is compensation for extra risk, not a market inefficiency waiting to be exploited by a retail investor with less information than the institutions who were also holding (and slowly exiting) the same paper.

Part 04Where Bonds Sit in the Capital Plan

The ₹15,000 overnight-fund buffer is NOT invested in bonds directly — it sits in an overnight/liquid mutual fund (holding very short-duration G-Secs and top-rated commercial paper internally), prioritising same-day-or-next-day liquidity over yield. True bond allocation — AAA corporate bonds or G-Secs held directly for multi-year duration — is a Phase 3+ conversation (Portfolio Construction, per the curriculum's Track G), not a near-term action item. Understanding the mechanics now means the eventual allocation decision is informed rather than reactive.
