Part 01Indices — The Market's Scoreboard

1.1 The Cricket Team Analogy

THE CRICKET TEAM ANALOGY: Nifty 50 is not "the market" — it's a hand-picked XI. NSE's index committee selects the 50 largest, most liquid companies by FREE-FLOAT MARKET CAP (more on that below) and re-drafts the squad twice a year (March and September). A stock that shrinks or loses liquidity gets dropped; a rising challenger gets added. When you hear "Nifty is up 1.2% today," you're hearing the WEIGHTED performance of these 50 players, not literally every listed company in India — there are over 5,000 of those. Sensex is BSE's older, 30-stock version of the same idea.

1.2 Free-Float Market Cap — Why It's Not Just "Company Size"

ConceptFormulaWhy It Matters
Total Market CapShare Price × Total Shares OutstandingRaw company size — includes promoter-locked shares
Free-Float Market CapShare Price × (Total Shares − Promoter/Locked Shares)What actually TRADES — this is what index weighting uses

A company with ₹5L crore total market cap but 75% promoter holding has only ₹1.25L crore of free-float — a company half its size with low promoter holding could carry similar or greater index weight. Free-float weighting exists because index funds need to actually be able to BUY the shares they're tracking; illiquid promoter-locked stock isn't purchasable at scale.

1.3 Index Rebalancing

Twice a year, NSE reviews the Nifty 50 constituent list against fresh free-float data. Additions and deletions cause real, mechanical price impact — every index fund and ETF tracking Nifty MUST buy the new entrant and sell the exiting stock on the effective date, regardless of view. This creates a predictable (if modest) flow event worth watching around announcement dates.

Part 02Market Cap Segments — SEBI's Official Buckets
SegmentSEBI Definition (by rank)Character
Large-CapRank 1–100 by full market capLower volatility, higher liquidity, institutional-heavy ownership, slower compounding
Mid-CapRank 101–250Higher growth potential, higher volatility, less analyst coverage — more mispricing opportunity
Small-CapRank 251 onwardHighest volatility, thinnest liquidity, highest fraud/governance risk, largest theoretical upside

THE LIQUIDITY TRAP IN SMALL-CAPS: A small-cap stock can look cheap on paper and still be a bad trade if the daily traded volume is thin. Buying ₹50,000 worth of a stock that trades ₹2L of volume a day means YOU are 25% of the day's activity — getting out later at a fair price becomes difficult, especially in a panic. Always check average daily volume (visible on Kite/TradingView) before sizing a small-cap position, not just the price chart.

Part 03FII/DII Flows — Reading the Big Money

3.1 Who They Are

FII (Foreign Institutional Investors)DII (Domestic Institutional Investors)
Foreign mutual funds, pension funds, sovereign wealth funds investing in Indian marketsIndian mutual funds, insurance companies (LIC being the largest), banks, pension funds
Flows driven by global risk appetite, US Fed rate decisions, dollar strength, emerging-market allocation trendsFlows driven substantially by domestic SIP inflows — a large, sticky, monthly source of buying
Historically the more volatile, sentiment-driving flow — FII selling has coincided with sharp Nifty correctionsActs as a stabilising counterweight — DII buying has repeatedly absorbed FII selling in recent years

THE TUG OF WAR ANALOGY: Picture Nifty as a rope. FIIs pull from one end, DIIs from the other. When FIIs sell aggressively and DIIs aren't buying enough to absorb it, the rope moves sharply down. The rise of India's own SIP culture over the last decade has made the DII end of the rope structurally heavier than it used to be — a genuine change in market character worth understanding, not just a talking point.

3.2 Where to Check This Daily

NSE publishes FII/DII net buy-sell figures after market close daily; the data is also mirrored on most broker terminals and financial news sites. A single day's number is noise — a persistent multi-day directional trend (5+ consecutive sessions of one-sided flow) is the signal worth acting on, particularly when it diverges from what price is doing (e.g., FIIs net-selling hard while Nifty grinds higher on DII support — a fragility worth flagging, not ignoring).

Part 04Shareholding Pattern — Reading the Ownership X-Ray

Every listed company discloses its shareholding pattern quarterly — who owns what percentage. This is a free, public governance and conviction signal, available on NSE's website or any screener tool.

CategoryWhat It Tells You
Promoter HoldingFounders/controlling family's stake. Rising promoter holding over time = insider conviction. Falling = worth investigating why.
Promoter Pledge %Shares pledged as loan collateral. High pledge (>20-25%) is a red flag — a stock price crash can trigger forced margin-call selling by the lender, worsening the crash.
Public / Retail HoldingShares held by individual investors like Rachit — highly fragmented, rarely a governance factor on its own.
FII / DII HoldingInstitutional conviction. Rising institutional holding often precedes broader market recognition of a story.

RACHIT'S RULE: Before any equity buy, a 30-second shareholding-pattern check is non-negotiable: promoter holding trend (rising/flat/falling) and pledge percentage. A falling promoter stake combined with rising pledge is one of the cleanest early-warning combinations in Indian markets — it has preceded multiple high-profile corporate blow-ups.
