Part 01What a REIT Actually Is

1.1 The Apartment Building Syndicate Analogy

THE APARTMENT BUILDING SYNDICATE ANALOGY: Buying physical real estate directly means finding ₹50L+, arranging a home loan, handling tenants, and being fully illiquid for years. A REIT (Real Estate Investment Trust) is the syndicate version — a professionally managed trust pools together large, income-generating commercial properties (office parks, malls, warehouses), and sells UNITS in that trust on the stock exchange, exactly like buying shares in a company. Buy REIT units for as little as one unit's price and you own a proportional slice of, say, an entire Grade-A office park in Bengaluru's tech corridor — fully liquid, tradeable in seconds on Zerodha Kite, with zero tenant-management headache.

1.2 What REITs Are Legally Required to Do

Indian REIT regulations (SEBI) mandate that at least 90% of net distributable cash flow must be paid out to unit holders, at least twice a year. This isn't a company choosing to be generous with dividends — it's a structural, regulatory requirement, which is why REITs typically carry meaningfully higher and more predictable payout yields than a typical equity share.

Part 03Distributions and Tax — Read Before Assuming It's "Just a Dividend"
ComponentTax Treatment
Dividend component of distributionTaxable at your income slab IF the REIT's underlying SPV opted for the concessional corporate tax regime; tax-free otherwise — check the REIT's specific structure
Interest componentTaxable at your income slab (30% for Rachit) — no exemption
Amortisation of SPV debt (capital return) componentTax-free at receipt, but REDUCES your cost basis — taxed later as capital gains when units are sold
Capital gains on selling unitsSTCG (<12 months) at slab rate; LTCG (>12 months) at 12.5% above ₹1.25L exemption — same structure as equity LTCG

THE MULTI-COMPONENT TRAP: A single REIT distribution payout is actually a BLEND of dividend, interest, and capital-return components — each taxed differently, and the exact split is disclosed by the REIT with every distribution notice, not fixed. Treating the entire payout as a simple tax-free or fully-taxable number without checking the breakdown is a common and costly beginner error.
