"Options are not complicated instruments. They are simply contracts about the future. You either believe the market will move — or it won't. That one belief determines your entire strategy family." Rachit's Trading Philosophy · 2026

DEBIT FAMILY — You Pay Premium

You are the buyer of the spread. You pay net premium upfront. Your maximum loss = the premium you paid. Your maximum gain is capped at the strike gap minus what you paid. Time works against you — every day that passes without the market moving costs you money (Theta decay). Use when you expect movement.

CREDIT FAMILY — You Collect Premium

You are the seller of the spread. Cash enters your account on day one. Your maximum gain = premium collected. Your maximum loss = (strike gap − premium collected) × lot size. Time works FOR you — every day the market stays still adds to your profit. Use when you expect stillness.

The Mildly Bearish / Neutral Premise

Your current view: "I don't think Nifty will rise much from here. It might drift sideways or mildly lower." The Bear Call Spread is built exactly for this.

Step 1 — The Story

Who Is on Each Side of This Trade?

Every options trade has two parties with opposite convictions. Understanding both sides makes your conviction sharper.

🏦 YOU — The Seller (Bear Call)

You look at the chart. Nifty is at 24,300. You see strong resistance at 24,500 — a prior swing high with heavy OI concentration. You say: "I don't think Nifty crosses 24,500 by expiry. I'll charge someone a fee for that right — and keep the fee if I'm right." You SELL the 24,500 CE at ₹90 and collect ₹5,850 immediately.

VS

🐂 THE BUYER — The Call Buyer

The buyer of the 24,500 CE thinks: "Nifty is going to break 24,500 and rally. Maybe to 25,000. If I'm right, I make multiples of what I pay." They pay you ₹90 for the right to profit from that move. They need Nifty to go above 24,555 (your breakeven) before they see any profit.

🛡️ YOUR HEDGE — The 24,800 CE You Buy

You're not reckless. What if there's surprise positive news and Nifty shoots to 25,200? Unlimited losses is not your game. So you also BUY the 24,800 CE for ₹35. This caps your maximum pain at the 24,800 level — no matter how far Nifty goes above 24,800, your losses stop at ₹15,925. The hedge costs ₹2,275 but buys you infinite peace of mind above 24,800. You're now officially a spread trader — defined risk, defined reward.

The Outcome You're Betting On: Nifty stays below 24,500 (ideally stays below 24,555). Both Calls expire worthless. The ₹5,850 you collected minus the ₹2,275 hedge cost = ₹3,575 is yours to keep. No action needed. Time simply passes in your favour.

VERDICT: 100pt spread → Max loss ₹4,550 → Within 20% cap ✓
300pt spread → Max loss ₹15,925 → Too large for ₹20k ✗
KEY INSIGHT: With ₹20k, always trade 100pt-wide spreads maximum.
Wider spreads need capital of ₹50,000+ to stay within risk rules.

⚖️ The R:R Context — Why 1:4.46 is Acceptable Here

Standard R:R wisdom says never risk more than you make. But credit spreads invert this logic deliberately. You risk ₹4,550 to make ₹1,950. That's 1:2.3 — still unfavourable on paper. But here's the math that makes it work over time: If your probability of winning is 70%, then in 10 trades you win 7 (×₹1,950 = ₹13,650) and lose 3 (×₹4,550 = ₹13,650). Barely breakeven. This is why you need your probability read to be accurate, VIX to be in your favour, and a clean resistance level.

The 300pt spread (₹3,575 vs ₹15,925) needs a win rate of 82%+ to break even. That's too demanding for a routine strategy. Stick to 100pt spreads with tight capital until you build experience.

Step 5 — Risk Guards

Stop Loss, Guards, and Murphy's Whole Number Rule

🛑 Stop Loss Rule — Credit Spread

Primary rule: Exit if the cost to close the spread exceeds 2× what you collected.

You collected ₹1,950 (100pt spread). Exit if closing debit > ₹3,900 (i.e., net loss of ₹1,950 = you've given back all your profit and are now bleeding).

Simpler rule: If net premium to close the spread reaches ₹60/unit (vs ₹30/unit collected), exit immediately. Don't wait for max loss.

🛑 Stop Loss Rule — Chart Trigger

Murphy's rule: If Nifty closes ABOVE your sold strike (24,500) on a daily candle with above-average volume — exit the trade. Don't wait for expiry.

Intra-day guard: If Nifty breaches 24,500 intraday by more than 50 points (i.e., touches 24,550), start preparing the closing order. Breakeven is at 24,555 — dangerously close.

📐 Murphy's Whole Number Rule — Why 24,500 and 25,000 Matter

John Murphy dedicates significant space in Chapter 4 to psychological support and resistance. Round numbers — ₹100, ₹500, ₹1,000 — act as invisible walls because human beings naturally place orders at clean numbers. Institutions write massive options at round strikes. Retail traders place stop losses at round numbers. For Nifty specifically:

  • 25,000 is the supreme round-number resistance — major institutional writing at this strike
  • 24,500 is the 500-point sub-level — significant in its own right
  • 24,000 is the next major round support below
  • When choosing your sold strike, always align it with a round number that has visible OI — you have institutional confirmation working with you

01
Bull Call Spread
Market View: Moderately bullish. Expects rise, not explosion.
DEBIT

The Story: You think Nifty will rise from 24,300 to somewhere around 24,700. But the 24,300 CE alone costs ₹180 — too expensive. So you make a deal: you'll cap your profit at 24,700 in exchange for selling the 24,700 CE to someone else for ₹60. The buyer of the 24,700 CE thinks Nifty will shoot past 24,700 and keeps going — they're the aggressive bull. You're the moderate bull. Both of you are wrong about each other's conviction. You pay a net ₹120. If Nifty rises between 24,420 and 24,700 — you profit. If it roars past 24,700 — you still make your capped maximum but miss the extra upside. The ₹60 credit from selling is literally a coupon off your ticket price.

Bull Call Spread · Full Calculation
BUY 24,300 CE @ ₹180 → ₹180 × 65 = ₹11,700 (paid)
SELL 24,700 CE @ ₹60 → ₹60 × 65 = ₹3,900 (collected)
Net Cost per unit = ₹180 − ₹60 = ₹120
Total Outlay = ₹120 × 65 = ₹7,800
Max Profit = (400 − 120) × 65 = ₹280 × 65 = ₹18,200
Max Loss = ₹120 × 65 = ₹7,800 (full premium)
Breakeven = 24,300 + 120 = 24,420
R:R Ratio = ₹18,200 ÷ ₹7,800 = 2.33 : 1

Risk Cap Check — ₹20,000 Options Capital

₹7,800 outlay = 39% of ₹20,000 options capital — exceeds 20% cap. To stay within ₹4,000 risk: use 100pt spread instead. Buy 24,300 CE @ ₹180, Sell 24,400 CE @ ₹135. Net cost ≈ ₹45/unit × 65 = ₹2,925. Within cap. Max profit = ₹55 × 65 = ₹3,575. R:R = 1.22:1. More modest but disciplined.

Stop Loss

Time-based exit: If 7 days pass and Nifty hasn't crossed 24,420 (breakeven), exit. Theta decay makes recovery unlikely. Don't let a debit spread die slowly.

Price-based exit: Exit if premium of the spread drops to 50% of what you paid. ₹7,800 paid → exit if spread value drops to ₹3,900.

  • Chart shows uptrend with bullish candle patterns (Murphy: hammer, morning star above support)
  • Strong support visible below current Nifty on chart
  • IV elevated — selling the upper Call meaningfully reduces cost
  • Avoid if market is at major resistance — rise may be limited
02
Bear Put Spread
Market View: Moderately bearish. Expects controlled fall.
DEBIT

The Story: Exact mirror of the Bull Call Spread — but for falls. You think Nifty will decline from 24,300 to around 23,900. Buying the 24,300 PE outright costs ₹175. Instead, you sell the 23,900 PE for ₹65 to someone who thinks Nifty will crash past 23,900 all the way to 23,000. You're the controlled bear; the 23,900 PE buyer is the crash bear. You cap your profit at 23,900 but reduce your entry cost from ₹175 to ₹110. If Nifty falls between 24,190 and 23,900 — you profit handsomely with a 2.6:1 R:R.

Bear Put Spread · Full Calculation
BUY 24,300 PE @ ₹175 → ₹175 × 65 = ₹11,375 (paid)
SELL 23,900 PE @ ₹65 → ₹65 × 65 = ₹4,225 (collected)
Net Cost per unit = ₹175 − ₹65 = ₹110
Total Outlay = ₹110 × 65 = ₹7,150
Max Profit = (400 − 110) × 65 = ₹290 × 65 = ₹18,850
Max Loss = ₹110 × 65 = ₹7,150
Breakeven = 24,300 − 110 = 24,190
R:R Ratio = ₹18,850 ÷ ₹7,150 = 2.64 : 1

Risk Cap Check

₹7,150 = 35.7% of ₹20k. Exceed cap. For 100pt spread: Buy 24,300 PE @ ₹175, Sell 24,200 PE @ ₹130. Net ≈ ₹45 × 65 = ₹2,925. Within ₹4,000 cap. Trade only 1 lot.

Stop Loss

Exit if Nifty closes ABOVE your bought Put strike (24,300) with strong bullish momentum. Your bearish thesis is invalidated. Exit if premium falls to 50% of entry value.

05
Long Straddle
Market View: Big move certain. Direction irrelevant.
EVENT

The Story: RBI policy announcement is tomorrow. Markets are on edge. You have no idea if they'll cut rates (bullish) or hold/hike (bearish) — but you're certain the market will move violently either way. You buy the 24,300 CE for ₹180 AND the 24,300 PE for ₹175 — ₹355 total. You're not guessing direction; you're betting on the magnitude of the move. If Nifty shoots to 25,000 — the Call prints massively and the Put expires worthless. If Nifty tanks to 23,500 — the Put wins and the Call is the sunk cost. Your enemy is not the direction — it's time. If RBI says nothing remarkable and Nifty barely moves, both options decay and you lose ₹23,075. Enter 1–2 days before the event only.

Long Straddle · Full Calculation
BUY 24,300 CE @ ₹180 → ₹180 × 65 = ₹11,700
BUY 24,300 PE @ ₹175 → ₹175 × 65 = ₹11,375
Total Cost = ₹355/unit × 65 = ₹23,075
Max Profit = Unlimited on Call side if Nifty explodes upward
Max Loss = ₹23,075 (if Nifty expires exactly at 24,300)
Upper Breakeven = 24,300 + 355 = 24,655
Lower Breakeven = 24,300 − 355 = 23,945
Move Needed = ±355 points = ±1.46% (achievable on events)
Theta Burn = ~₹45−60 per day on combined position

Risk Cap Check

₹23,075 far exceeds ₹4,000 cap. Straddle is NOT suitable for ₹20,000 options capital. Use Strangle instead (₹10,725). Or wait until capital grows to ₹75,000+.

Stop Loss

Time stop: If the event passes and Nifty hasn't moved beyond either breakeven within 1–2 days of the event — exit both legs immediately. Don't hold through post-event IV crush. IV will collapse (VIX drops) after the event — destroying the remaining premium.

06
Long Strangle
Market View: Big extreme move coming. Budget version of Straddle.
EVENT

The Story: Same event premise as the Straddle — but you're working with ₹20k and can't afford ₹23,075. So you shift both options OUT of the money. Instead of buying the ATM 24,300 CE/PE, you buy the 24,600 CE (above market) and the 24,000 PE (below market). You pay only ₹165/unit instead of ₹355 — a saving of ₹12,350. The catch: Nifty needs to move further (±465 points vs ±355 in the straddle) before you profit. You're paying for a bigger swing. This makes the Strangle ideal for budget days, election results, or US Fed events — scenarios where 2–3% moves are common.

Long Strangle · Full Calculation
BUY 24,600 CE @ ₹85 → ₹85 × 65 = ₹5,525
BUY 24,000 PE @ ₹80 → ₹80 × 65 = ₹5,200
Total Cost = ₹165/unit × 65 = ₹10,725
Max Profit = Unlimited on either side
Max Loss = ₹10,725
Upper Breakeven = 24,600 + 165 = 24,765 (+1.9% move)
Lower Breakeven = 24,000 − 165 = 23,835 (−1.9% move)
Saving vs Straddle = ₹23,075 − ₹10,725 = ₹12,350

Risk Cap Check

₹10,725 = 53.6% of ₹20k. Still exceeds conservative 20% cap. However for event trades, many traders accept 1 lot of strangle as a contained unit. Treat the full premium as your max loss and size accordingly. If ₹10k is too much, skip the event.

Stop Loss

Exit within 24 hours of the event announcement. OTM options decay even faster than ATM after IV crush. If neither breakeven is breached within 1 day of the event, the trade is unlikely to recover.

07
Iron Condor
Market View: Range bound. No major events. Income from stillness.
CREDIT

The Story: No RBI this week. No elections. No Fed. Nifty has been coiling between 24,000 and 24,600 for 10 days. You see it clearly on the chart — a defined range with tested support and resistance. Instead of guessing which way it breaks, you charge a toll from both sides simultaneously. You sell a Bear Call Spread above (24,600/24,900) AND a Bull Put Spread below (24,000/23,700). Four legs. Two premiums collected on each side. Nifty must stay between 24,000 and 24,600 for you to keep all ₹6,500. It's the weekly paycheck strategy used by professional Indian options traders — the "toll booth model." The only way you lose meaningfully is if something unexpected breaks the range.

Iron Condor · Full 4-Leg Calculation
UPPER SPREAD (Bear Call):
SELL 24,600 CE @ ₹90 → ₹90 × 65 = ₹5,850
BUY 24,900 CE @ ₹35 → ₹35 × 65 = ₹2,275
Upper spread credit = ₹55 × 65 = ₹3,575

LOWER SPREAD (Bull Put):
SELL 24,000 PE @ ₹85 → ₹85 × 65 = ₹5,525
BUY 23,700 PE @ ₹40 → ₹40 × 65 = ₹2,600
Lower spread credit = ₹45 × 65 = ₹2,925
Total Net Credit = (₹55 + ₹45) × 65 = ₹100 × 65 = ₹6,500
Max Profit = ₹6,500 (Nifty between 24,000 and 24,600)
Max Loss (either side) = (300 − 100) × 65 = ₹13,000
Upper Breakeven = 24,600 + 100 = 24,700
Lower Breakeven = 24,000 − 100 = 23,900
Profitable zone = 23,900 to 24,700 (800 point runway)

Risk Cap Check

Max loss ₹13,000 = 65% of ₹20k. This strategy requires full ₹20k dedicated to margins plus buffer. Not ideal for ₹20k. More appropriate at ₹50,000+ options capital. Build toward this — it's the pinnacle income strategy.

Stop Loss

Leg-by-leg rule: If Nifty breaches either inner strike (24,600 or 24,000) and closes there — close the breached spread immediately. Keep the profitable spread open. Do NOT hold both if one side is blown.

08
Covered Call
Market View: Already own stock/ETF. Want monthly income on holdings.
CREDIT

The Story: You own the Motilal Oswal Midcap Fund or Nifty ETF. The units sit there. Instead of letting them earn nothing beyond NAV appreciation, you rent them out. You sell a Call option against your holdings — pocketing ₹5,850/month in premium for agreeing that if Nifty rises above 24,600, you'll sell your units at that price. You're happy to sell at 24,600 — it's profit. If Nifty doesn't reach 24,600 by expiry, the Call expires worthless and you keep the ₹5,850 without selling anything. The next month you do it again. Warren Buffett generates hundreds of millions annually this way on Berkshire's existing portfolio. This is the closest thing to a rent cheque from your investments.

Covered Call · Full Calculation
POSITION: Hold 65 units of Nifty ETF @ ₹24,300
VALUE OF HOLDING = 65 × ₹24,300 = ₹15,79,500

SELL 24,600 CE @ ₹90 → ₹90 × 65 = ₹5,850 per month
Monthly income rate = ₹5,850 ÷ ₹15,79,500 = 0.37% per month
Annualised yield = ~4.4% — on top of NAV appreciation

SCENARIO A — Nifty below 24,600 at expiry:
Call expires worthless. Keep ₹5,850. Still own units. Repeat.

SCENARIO B — Nifty above 24,600 at expiry:
Units are called away at ₹24,600. Profit = ₹300/unit × 65 + ₹5,850 = ₹25,350
You miss any upside beyond 24,600. That's the tradeoff.
  • You hold the underlying — this is non-negotiable for the strategy to be "covered"
  • You are willing to sell your holding at the Call strike (no emotional attachment)
  • Flat or mildly bullish outlook on your holding
  • Avoid during strong bull phases — you'll repeatedly miss the upside

⚠️ The Invisible Hand — Algo Trading, Market Manipulation & Jane Street

You are not trading against other retail traders. You are trading against machines designed by the world's best mathematicians and programmed to exploit every pattern, every hesitation, every behavioural flaw. Knowing this is not meant to paralyse you — it's meant to make you sharper and more patient.

"In 2024, SEBI issued notices to Jane Street — a global high-frequency trading and market-making firm — over alleged manipulation of Indian index options. The allegation was that Jane Street was taking coordinated positions in Nifty component stocks to artificially move the Nifty 50 index level, then profiting from related options positions. SEBI temporarily barred Jane Street from Indian securities markets, marking one of the most significant regulatory actions against algorithmic traders in India's history. This was not isolated — it was a window into how large firms interact with retail options markets every day."

1. Strike Pinning — The Expiry Day Gravity Well

Near expiry, Nifty exhibits an uncanny tendency to "pin" to round-number strikes. This is not coincidence. Market makers with large short option books have a direct incentive to push price toward the strike where the most options expire worthless — minimising their payout. If large open interest sits at 24,500 and it's Thursday morning of expiry week, watch Nifty drift toward 24,500 as if magnetised.

  • Watch For Slow grind toward a high-OI strike in the last hour of expiry trading
  • Watch For Sudden reversal right at your sold strike — manipulation protecting a level
  • Your Edge Choose strike levels well above/below current price with clear OI support

2. Spoofing — The Ghost Order

Spoofing means placing large orders (often thousands of lots) at key levels with no intention of executing them. The goal is to create false impression of supply/demand — pushing price toward or away from a target — then cancelling the order once price has moved. These orders appear and vanish within milliseconds. As a retail trader, you see them as "big walls" in the options chain that disappear the moment price approaches.

  • Watch For Very large bid/ask walls in options chain that vanish as price nears them
  • Watch For Sudden OI change mid-session without corresponding price move
  • Your Edge Only trade when OI builds over multiple sessions — not intraday OI spikes

3. IV Manipulation Before Events

Implied Volatility (and thus option premiums) can be artificially inflated before known events (RBI, budget, earnings). Institutional players accumulate options quietly, then sell into retail demand as IV spikes. After the event, IV collapses (the "volatility crush") and all those expensive options retail traders bought at peak IV are suddenly worth far less — even if the direction was right.

  • Watch For India VIX spiking aggressively in the 5 days before a known event
  • Watch For Straddle premium becoming unusually expensive relative to historical averages
  • Your Edge For event trades: enter as CLOSE to the event as possible to avoid inflated IV

4. Gamma Squeeze / Delta Hedging Cascade

When Nifty approaches a large option strike near expiry, market makers who are short calls must buy more Nifty to stay delta-neutral (hedged). This buying itself pushes Nifty higher — which forces more buying — creating a self-reinforcing cascade. Algos detect and front-run this precisely. Retail sees "Nifty suddenly shot up for no reason." The reason is a Gamma squeeze engineered by algorithmic hedging.

  • Watch For Nifty accelerating toward a round number with no clear news catalyst
  • Watch For The move stopping sharply exactly at a high-OI strike
  • Your Edge Don't chase these moves. Let them come to you. Set your strikes conservatively.

5. Last-Hour Marking / Settlement Manipulation

Options settle at the Special Opening Quotation (SOQ) or closing price depending on contract terms. Large players can manipulate closing prices in thinly traded components to move the index settlement price by fractions that make the difference between millions in profit or loss on massive option books. Retail traders should never make critical expiry decisions in the last 15 minutes of trading.

  • Watch For Unusually high volume and erratic price action in last 30 minutes of expiry sessions
  • Watch For Gap between Nifty spot and Nifty futures in the final minutes
  • Your Edge Close your expiry-day positions by 3:00 PM, not at 3:30 PM. Never hold to settlement.

6. Quote Stuffing and Latency Arbitrage

HFT firms flood exchanges with millions of fake orders per second to cause congestion, slowing down competitors' order routing systems. In those microseconds of lag, they execute arbitrage trades that retail traders never see. While this doesn't directly harm your spread positions, it does mean the "prices you see" on screen can briefly diverge from true price during volatility spikes — making limit orders essential over market orders in options.

  • Watch For Bid-ask spreads widening suddenly during news events
  • Your Edge Always use LIMIT orders in options. Never market orders. Slippage can be brutal.
THE RETAIL TRADER'S ADVANTAGE: Algos optimise for milliseconds. You optimise for days and weeks. You can afford to wait for the perfect setup. Algos can't. Your edge is patience and selectivity — theirs is speed and scale. Never try to out-trade an algorithm on its own turf (scalping, market orders, chasing moves). Trade on your own turf: planned entries, defined risk, strategic setups.
