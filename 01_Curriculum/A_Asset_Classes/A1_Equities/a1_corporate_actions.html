Part 01Dividends — Getting Paid to Hold

1.1 The Landlord Analogy

THE LANDLORD ANALOGY: Owning a share is like owning a small slice of a rental property. Most months the property just sits there appreciating quietly — but periodically the "landlord" (the company's board) decides to distribute some of the accumulated rent (profit) directly to owners. That distribution is the DIVIDEND. Not every company pays one — high-growth companies often reinvest 100% of profit back into the business instead, which is not a red flag by itself.

1.2 The Three Dates That Actually Matter

DateWhat It MeansPractical Impact
Announcement DateBoard declares the dividend amount per shareInformational only
Record DateCompany checks its register — whoever owns shares on this date gets paidMiss this and you get nothing, even if you sell the next day
Ex-Dividend DateFirst day the stock trades WITHOUT the dividend attached (in India, ex-date = record date under T+1)Buy on or after this date and you will NOT receive the upcoming dividend

THE EX-DATE PRICE DROP — NOT A CRASH: On the ex-dividend date, the stock price mechanically drops by approximately the dividend amount at market open. A ₹500 stock paying a ₹10 dividend opens near ₹490. This is NOT the market punishing the company — the company's cash reserves just dropped by the amount paid out, and the share price reflects that instantly. Confusing this mechanical adjustment with a bearish signal is a classic beginner misread.

1.3 Dividend Yield — The Number That Actually Compares

Dividend Yield = (Annual Dividend Per Share ÷ Current Share Price) × 100. A ₹500 stock paying ₹15/year in dividends yields 3%. This is the number to compare ACROSS stocks — comparing raw rupee dividend amounts between a ₹50 stock and a ₹2,000 stock is meaningless without adjusting for price.

Part 02Stock Splits — More Slices, Same Cake

2.1 The Pizza Slice Analogy

THE PIZZA SLICE ANALOGY: A 1:5 stock split takes one ₹5,000 slice of pizza and cuts it into five ₹1,000 slices. You now hold 5x the shares at 1/5th the price each — the total value of your holding is UNCHANGED on split day. Nothing about the company's actual business, profit, or assets changed; only the number of slices the same pizza is cut into.

Why companies split: primarily to lower the per-share price and improve retail affordability/liquidity — a ₹15,000 share deters small investors from buying even a single share, while a post-split ₹1,500 share doesn't. Splits carry zero direct tax event in India; your cost basis simply divides proportionally across the new share count.

Part 03Bonus Shares — Free Slices, Not Free Money

3.1 How Bonus Differs From a Split

AspectStock SplitBonus Issue
MechanismFace value of existing shares is dividedNew shares issued FREE, funded from company reserves
Typical Ratio1:5, 1:10 (five/ten new shares per one old)1:1, 2:1 (one/two new shares per one old)
Accounting SourceNo reserves used — pure face-value divisionMoves money from Reserves to Share Capital on the balance sheet
Investor ImpactValue unchanged, more/cheaper sharesValue unchanged, more shares — same net effect from the investor's chair

WHY "FREE SHARES" ISN'T FREE MONEY: A 1:1 bonus means a ₹1,000 stock becomes two shares worth ₹500 each — you now hold 2 shares worth ₹1,000 total instead of 1 share worth ₹1,000. Nothing was created. The psychological appeal of "more shares in my demat" is real but the arithmetic is neutral. The genuine signal worth reading is WHY the company chose to reward shareholders this way — often paired with genuine confidence in sustained future earnings, since the company is committing to sustain dividend-per-share on a now-larger share count.

Part 04Buybacks — The Company Buying Its Own Slices Back

4.1 Tender Offer vs. Open Market Buyback

MethodMechanicsRachit's Access
Tender OfferCompany sets a fixed buyback price (usually at a premium to market) and a fixed window; shareholders can tender shares proportionallyApply through Kite during the tender window if holding the stock — acceptance is often prorated if oversubscribed
Open Market BuybackCompany buys shares gradually from the open exchange over months, like any other buyerNo special action needed — you simply may be one of the sellers on the other side of company purchases

4.2 Why Buybacks Happen and What They Signal

THE SHRINKING PIZZA, SAME TOPPINGS ANALOGY: A buyback reduces the total number of outstanding shares — the same company profit is now divided among fewer slices, mechanically raising Earnings Per Share (EPS) even if actual profit doesn't grow a rupee. Management often frames buybacks as "we believe our stock is undervalued" — sometimes true, sometimes a way to return cash without the ongoing commitment a dividend implies (dividends are expected to continue; buybacks are one-off). A buyback funded by taking on new DEBT rather than genuine free cash reserves is a yellow flag worth checking in the announcement details.

Part 05Rights Issues — Buy More or Get Diluted

5.1 The Mechanics

A rights issue offers EXISTING shareholders the right to buy additional shares, typically at a discount to market price, in a fixed ratio (e.g., 1:4 — one new share for every four held).

ChoiceConsequence
Subscribe (buy your allotted rights shares)Maintain your percentage ownership in the company; deploy fresh capital at a discount
Let it lapse (do nothing)Your percentage ownership gets DILUTED as new shares enter circulation; in India, rights entitlements can also be sold on the exchange during the window, recovering some value instead of an outright lapse

RACHIT'S EVALUATION RULE: A rights issue used to fund genuine growth capex or debt reduction, priced at a real discount, from a company with a track record of disciplined capital allocation — reasonable case to subscribe. A rights issue from a company that's been burning cash and repeatedly returning to shareholders for fresh capital is a pattern worth treating as a warning, not a buying opportunity, regardless of the discount offered.
