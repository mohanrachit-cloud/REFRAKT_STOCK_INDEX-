Part 01What Commodity Trading Actually Is

1.1 The Warehouse Receipt Analogy

THE WAREHOUSE RECEIPT ANALOGY: A commodity futures contract is a standardised promise tied to a physical warehouse receipt — one contract of Gold on MCX represents a specific, exchange-defined quantity (e.g. 100 grams for Gold Mini) of a specific purity, deliverable from an accredited vault. You are not buying a jewellery-shop transaction; you are buying a financial contract whose price tracks that physical commodity, almost always closed out in cash before delivery ever becomes relevant to a retail trader.

Part 02Why This Isn't a Near-Term Allocation

THE HONEST CAPITAL CHECK: Even the smallest "Mini" commodity contracts carry margin requirements in the ₹15,000-45,000 range per lot — against a total starting capital under ₹1L, with ₹60k already earmarked for equity, ₹20k for options, and ₹15k locked as an overnight buffer. There simply isn't a clean, undedicated pocket of capital to deploy into commodities without cannibalising an allocation that's already doing a specific job. This mirrors the exact capital-constraint logic that ruled out Nifty Futures in favour of currency futures — the lesson generalises: instrument choice is downstream of capital reality, not the other way around.

2.1 What Commodities Are Useful For, Conceptually, Even Before Trading Them

Gold in particular functions as a portfolio diversifier — it has historically moved with low or negative correlation to Indian equities during risk-off periods, which is precisely why Sovereign Gold Bonds (covered in the Bonds module) are a more capital-efficient way to get gold exposure right now than an MCX futures contract: no daily margin/MTM management, a 2.5% coupon on top of gold's price move, and eventual tax-free maturity. Direct commodity futures trading is worth returning to once capital comfortably exceeds the current allocation plan, not before.
