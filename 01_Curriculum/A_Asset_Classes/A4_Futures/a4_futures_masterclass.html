Part 01What Exactly Is a Futures Contract?

1.1 The Wheat Farmer Analogy — Where Futures Were Born

Imagine you are a wheat farmer in Punjab. You are planting wheat today and will harvest it in 3 months. But wheat prices are volatile — if the price crashes before harvest, you lose everything. So you make a deal TODAY with a flour mill in Delhi:

FARMER says: I will sell you 100 quintals of wheat at Rs. 2,000 per quintal, delivered in 3 months. MILL says: Agreed. We will pay Rs. 2,000 per quintal in 3 months, no matter what the market price is. This signed, standardised agreement traded on an exchange = A FUTURES CONTRACT. Farmer = Seller (Short position) — locks in selling price, hedges against price fall Mill = Buyer (Long position) — locks in buying price, hedges against price rise Rs. 2,000/quintal = Futures Price (agreed today for future delivery) 3 months = Contract Expiry (when the deal settles)

In modern markets, the 'wheat' could be a stock index, a company's shares, a currency, crude oil, or gold. The principle is identical: lock in a price today for a transaction that happens in the future.

1.3 Futures vs. Options — The Critical Distinction

FUTURES = Obligation. Both buyer AND seller MUST complete the transaction. OPTIONS = Right without obligation. The BUYER has a choice. Seller is always obligated.

ANALOGY: Futures = Signing a rental agreement. You MUST pay rent every month. No exit without penalty. Options = Paying token money to book a flat. You have the RIGHT to buy. If the deal sours, you just lose the token money. The seller must still honour if you want to buy.

PRACTICAL IMPACT: Futures are symmetric — gain Rs. 50,000 if market moves 500 points in your favour. Lose Rs. 50,000 if market moves 500 points against you. Options are asymmetric — maximum loss is premium paid (for buyers).

2.2 Margin — The Engine of Futures

Margin is what makes futures both powerful and dangerous. You control a large contract by paying only a fraction of its value. This is leverage — the financial equivalent of a lever that amplifies both gains AND losses.

THE LEVER ANALOGY: A 10-foot lever lets you lift a 1,000 kg rock by applying just 100 kg of force. You get 10x the lifting power — but if the rock falls, it falls with 10x the force too. Futures Margin Example (Nifty @ 23,000): Contract Value = 50 x 23,000 = Rs. 11,50,000 SPAN Margin required = ~Rs. 1,20,000 (approx 10.4%) Exposure Margin = ~Rs. 57,500 (additional 5%) Total Margin = ~Rs. 1,77,500 If Nifty moves from 23,000 to 23,500 (+500 points): Your P&L = 500 x 50 = Rs. 25,000 PROFIT Return on margin = 25,000 / 1,77,500 = 14% on ONE 500-point move Return on full contract value = 25,000 / 11,50,000 = 2.2% LEVERAGE AMPLIFIED your return by ~6.5x

2.3 Types of Margin — You Must Know All Three

CRITICAL: Mark-to-Market (MTM) is the concept that separates experienced futures traders from beginners. With stocks: You can ignore paper losses. Your broker does nothing. With futures: Losses are COLLECTED FROM YOU EVERY EVENING. Example: You are LONG 1 Nifty Futures lot at 23,000. Day 1: Nifty closes at 22,700 (-300 points). Your MTM loss = 300 x 50 = Rs. 15,000. Zerodha DEBITS Rs. 15,000 from your account at end of day. Day 2: If your account balance falls below maintenance margin, you get a MARGIN CALL. You must add funds OR your position is SQUARED OFF by the broker. This is why futures require a cash buffer beyond just the initial margin.

2.4 The MTM P&L Formula — Know This Cold

For LONG Futures (you bought): P&L = (Current Price - Entry Price) x Lot Size For SHORT Futures (you sold): P&L = (Entry Price - Current Price) x Lot Size Example 1 - LONG Nifty at 23,000, Current price 23,400: P&L = (23,400 - 23,000) x 50 = 400 x 50 = Rs. 20,000 PROFIT Example 2 - SHORT Reliance Futures at 2,800, Current price 2,950: P&L = (2,800 - 2,950) x 250 = -150 x 250 = Rs. 37,500 LOSS Remember: Lot sizes differ per contract. Always check NSE's contract specification.

Part 03Futures Pricing — Why Is the Futures Price Different from Spot?

3.1 The Cost of Carry Model

The spot price is what something costs RIGHT NOW. The futures price is what you agree to pay for it LATER. They are almost never the same — and understanding WHY is the foundation of all futures analysis.

THE HOTEL BOOKING ANALOGY: Room price today (walk-in): Rs. 5,000/night = SPOT PRICE Room price if you book 3 months ahead: Rs. 5,200/night = FUTURES PRICE The Rs. 200 extra = Cost of Carry (the hotel needs to hold the room for you) In futures markets, cost of carry = interest cost of holding the asset + storage/dividend adjustments

FORMULA: Futures Price = Spot Price x (1 + r)^(T/365) Where r = risk-free rate (approx 6.5% in India, 2026), T = days to expiry Example: Nifty Spot = 23,000, Days to expiry = 30, Risk-free rate = 6.5% Fair Futures Price = 23,000 x (1 + 0.065)^(30/365) = 23,000 x 1.00534 = 23,123

3.2 Premium, Discount and Basis — The Three States

STATEWHAT IT MEANSSIGNAL
Normal (Contango)Futures price > Spot price. Futures at PREMIUM.Normal market. Carry cost + optimism priced in.
BackwardationFutures price < Spot price. Futures at DISCOUNT.Bearish signal. Sellers dominate futures. Watch carefully.
Fair ValueFutures = Spot + Carry Cost exactlyMarket in equilibrium — no edge either way.

BASIS = Spot Price - Futures Price Positive basis = Spot > Futures = Backwardation (unusual, watch for distress) Negative basis = Futures > Spot = Normal/Contango (usual state)

BASIS CONVERGENCE: As expiry approaches, futures price MUST converge to spot. On expiry day: Futures price = Spot price. Always. No exceptions. This convergence is mechanical — built into the settlement process. Murphy's insight: Extreme premium or discount in futures signals institutional imbalance. A sharp rise in futures premium = institutions aggressively buying ahead of a catalyst.

3.3 Open Interest — The Pulse of the Futures Market

Open Interest (OI) is the total number of outstanding futures contracts that have NOT been settled. It is the single most important data point in futures markets — more important than volume.

THE PARTY ANALOGY: Imagine a dance floor. Every pair of dancers = 1 futures contract (1 buyer + 1 seller). Open Interest = Number of pairs currently on the dance floor. Volume = Total number of pairs who danced at any point today. When new people enter and dance = OI increases (new contracts created) When pairs leave = OI decreases (contracts closed) When one dancer swaps partner = Volume increases, OI stays same (position transfer)

PRICE CHANGEOI CHANGESIGNALINTERPRETATION
RisingRisingBULLISH (Strong)New longs entering. Trend has conviction. Murphy: strong buy signal.
RisingFallingCAUTIONShort sellers covering. Rally may lack conviction.
FallingRisingBEARISH (Strong)New shorts entering. Downtrend has conviction.
FallingFallingWATCHLongs exiting. Decline may be overdone — reversal possible.

4.3 The Expiry Cycle — India's 3-Series System

India's futures market always has THREE contract series live simultaneously: Near Month (current month), Mid Month (next month), Far Month (month after). Each expires on the last Thursday of its respective month.

EXAMPLE (April 2026 context): Near Month: April 2026 contract (expires last Thursday of April) Mid Month: May 2026 contract Far Month: June 2026 contract On expiry Thursday: Near month expires. Mid becomes Near. Far becomes Mid. A new Far Month contract (July) is launched.

LIQUIDITY RULE: Near month always has 90%+ of total volume and OI. Trade near month unless you have a specific reason to go to mid or far month.

4.4 Rollover — Moving Your Position to the Next Month

WHAT IS ROLLOVER? If you hold a futures position and do NOT want to let it expire, you ROLL it over. This means: Close your near-month position + Open the same position in mid-month.

ROLLOVER ANALOGY: Like renewing your electricity contract every month instead of letting it lapse. You cancel this month's contract and sign next month's — same deal, different date. ROLLOVER COST = Mid-month price - Near-month price Normal market: Positive rollover cost (mid is at premium to near) Bearish signal: Negative rollover cost (mid is at discount to near)

ROLLOVER PERIOD: Typically 3-5 days before expiry (3rd week of month). Watch rollover data to understand institutional positioning for next month.

Part 06Futures Trading Strategies

6.1 Directional Strategies — The Basics

Strategy 1: Trend Following (Murphy Chapter 4 — Primary Trend)

WHEN TO USE: Nifty in a clear uptrend (higher highs + higher lows on daily chart)

ENTRY: Buy Nifty Futures on pullback to key support (50 EMA or prior resistance turned support)

STOP LOSS: Below the prior swing low (below support level)

TARGET: Prior resistance level or 1.618 Fibonacci extension EXAMPLE (Illustrative): Nifty in uptrend. Pulls back to 22,500 (50 EMA support). Enter LONG 1 lot. Stop loss: 22,200 (prior swing low). Risk = 300 points x 50 = Rs. 15,000. Target: 23,200 (prior resistance). Reward = 700 points x 50 = Rs. 35,000. Risk-Reward = 1:2.3 — acceptable.

Strategy 2: Breakout Trading

WHEN TO USE: Nifty breaks above key resistance with high volume AND rising

OI ENTRY: Buy futures on breakout candle close above resistance (confirmed close, not just wick)

STOP LOSS: Below the breakout level (if it reverses below, the breakout was false)

TARGET: Measured move = Height of prior consolidation range added to breakout point

MURPHY RULE: Volume must confirm the breakout. High volume + OI expansion = genuine break. Low volume breakout = suspect. Wait for retest of breakout level before entering.

Strategy 3: Portfolio Hedging with Short Futures

SCENARIO: You own equity stocks/MFs worth Rs. 10 lakh. Market looks bearish. Instead of selling all holdings, you SHORT Nifty futures.

CALCULATION: Portfolio value: Rs. 10,00,000 Nifty Futures contract value: 23,000 x 50 = Rs. 11,50,000 Contracts to short = 10,00,000 / 11,50,000 = 0.87 => Short 1 lot (round down) If Nifty falls 1,000 points: Loss on portfolio (approx): ~Rs. 43,500 (if portfolio beta = 1) Gain on short futures: 1,000 x 50 = Rs. 50,000 Net result: Nearly flat. You protected your portfolio. IMPORTANT: Remove the hedge when bullish signal returns. Hedge costs carry (rollover).

6.2 Calendar Spread — The Direction-Neutral Strategy

A calendar spread involves simultaneously buying one month's futures and selling another month's futures on the SAME underlying. You are betting on the DIFFERENCE between contract prices, not on price direction.

EXAMPLE: Nifty April futures at 23,100. Nifty May futures at 23,220. Spread = 120 points. If you believe spread will

WIDEN: Buy May, Sell April If you believe spread will

NARROW: Buy April, Sell May KEY POINT: Calendar spreads have LOWER margin requirements because risk is bounded. NSE recognises the offsetting nature and gives margin relief.

RACHIT NOTE: This strategy is direction-neutral — you don't need to predict whether Nifty goes up or down. You only need to predict the spread. Useful during uncertain markets. (From our earlier conversation — this is the strategy we discussed as 'calendar spread'.)

Part 08Risk Management for Futures

8.1 The Non-Negotiable Rules

RULE 1 — THE 2% RULE: Never risk more than 2% of your total capital on a single futures trade. Your capital: Under Rs. 1 lakh. Max risk per trade = Rs. 2,000. If one Nifty futures lot risks Rs. 15,000 per your stop loss — it's too big. Don't trade it. RULE 2 — ALWAYS USE STOP LOSS ORDERS: Place your stop loss order IMMEDIATELY after entering. Not 'I will watch and exit manually'. Futures MTM can move against you very fast. Manual watching = emotional decisions. RULE 3 — MAINTAIN CASH BUFFER: Keep minimum 20% extra cash beyond your margin requirement in your account. This prevents forced square-offs on temporary adverse moves. RULE 4 — NEVER AVERAGE LOSING FUTURES: If your Nifty futures position is down 200 points and you buy another lot — you are doubling into a loss. Futures can go much further against you. Exit and re-evaluate.

Part 10Tax Treatment of Futures in India (30% Slab)

10.1 The Core Tax Rule — F&O is Business Income

CRITICAL TAX PRINCIPLE FOR INDIA: Futures & Options profits are ALWAYS treated as BUSINESS INCOME — NOT capital gains. This applies regardless of how frequently or infrequently you trade. This has profound implications: 1. Taxed at your INCOME TAX SLAB — 30% for Rachit 2. If

PROFIT: Add to total income. Tax at 30%. 3. If

LOSS: Can be SET OFF against business income (like freelance/Refrakt income) 4.

CARRY FORWARD: F&O losses can be carried forward for 8 years 5.

TAX AUDIT: Required if turnover exceeds Rs. 10 crore (or Rs. 1 crore pre-2024 limit) 6.

PRESUMPTIVE TAXATION: If turnover below Rs. 2 crore, can declare 6% as presumptive income

10.2 Calculating Turnover for F&O Tax

F&O TURNOVER DEFINITION (for tax purposes): Turnover = Absolute sum of all profits AND losses on each trade (NOT the contract values, just the net P&L on each transaction) Example: Trade 1: Nifty Long — Profit Rs. 25,000 → Turnover: Rs. 25,000 Trade 2: Nifty Short — Loss Rs. 15,000 → Turnover: Rs. 15,000 Trade 3: USD/INR Long — Profit Rs. 3,000 → Turnover: Rs. 3,000 Total Turnover = 25,000 + 15,000 + 3,000 = Rs. 43,000 For most retail traders at this level, turnover stays well below audit thresholds.

Part 11Ready-to-Trade Checklist

11.1 Pre-Trade Checklist — Before Every Futures Position

CHECK 1: Is the trend clear? (Daily chart — is price above or below 50 EMA?)

CHECK 2: What does OI data say? (Rising OI confirms trend; falling OI warns of reversal)

CHECK 3: Is the futures premium/discount making sense? (Extreme discount = warning)

CHECK 4: What is FII positioning? (FII net long or short in index futures?)

CHECK 5: Is your stop loss placed? (Before you click buy/sell)

CHECK 6: Does this trade risk less than 2% of your capital? CHECK 7: Is your margin account funded with 20% buffer above minimum margin? CHECK 8: What product type? MIS (intraday) or NRML (overnight)? Are you clear on this? CHECK 9: Is there an RBI announcement, Budget, or major event this week? CHECK 10: Do you have a specific, written reason for this trade — not just a 'feeling'?

11.2 Post-Trade Monitoring Checklist

DAILY (after market close): Check MTM P&L — will there be a margin call tomorrow? Check OI change — is the trend still confirmed by OI? Is your stop loss still valid, or should it be trailed up? WEEKLY: Review rollover data — are institutions rolling positions aggressively? Check Put-Call Ratio — is sentiment shifting? Review FII F&O data — institutional direction changing? PRE-EXPIRY (last 3 trading days of month): Decide: Exit, roll over, or let expire? Check the cost of carry for the next month's contract. Execute rollover before the last day to avoid liquidity issues.

11.3 The Futures Mental Model — Carry With You Always

FUTURES IN ONE PARAGRAPH: A futures contract is a standardised agreement to buy or sell an asset at a fixed price on a fixed future date. You control a large position (Rs. 10 lakh+) by paying only a margin (Rs. 1-2 lakh). Daily MTM ensures losses are collected immediately — you cannot ignore a bad position the way you can with stocks. Price = Spot + Cost of Carry. The futures price converges to spot at expiry. Rising price + Rising OI = Trend confirmed. F&O profits are taxed as business income at 30%. Losses can offset other business income. Start with currency futures (USD/INR) given current capital level. Scale to Nifty futures when capital exceeds Rs. 3-4 lakh. Always. Stop loss. Always.
