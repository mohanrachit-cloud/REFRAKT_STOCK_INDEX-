Part 01Bitcoin and Ethereum — The Foundation Layer

1.1 The Digital Gold vs. Digital Oil Analogy

THE DIGITAL GOLD / DIGITAL OIL ANALOGY: Bitcoin functions as DIGITAL GOLD — a fixed-supply (21 million coins, hard-capped, ever) store-of-value asset with no earnings, no dividends, no underlying cash flow — its entire investment case rests on scarcity and adoption, exactly like physical gold's case rests on scarcity and trust. Ethereum functions closer to DIGITAL OIL — a consumable resource that powers a global, programmable computing network (smart contracts, DeFi, NFTs all run "on" Ethereum, paying "gas fees" in ETH the way a car burns fuel to run). Different assets, different theses — lumping them together as "crypto" obscures more than it reveals.

1.2 What Actually Backs the Price

Unlike a share (backed by a company's earnings) or a bond (backed by a contractual repayment promise), Bitcoin and Ethereum are backed by nothing but network consensus and market belief in their utility/scarcity. This isn't automatically disqualifying — gold itself has no cash flow either — but it does mean traditional valuation tools (P/E ratios, DCF models) from the equity track simply don't apply here. Price is a pure function of supply/demand and narrative, making crypto structurally more volatile than equity or bonds.

Part 02Custody — The Single Biggest Risk Most New Investors Miss

2.1 "Not Your Keys, Not Your Coins"

THE BANK LOCKER VS. HOME SAFE ANALOGY: Holding crypto on an exchange (WazirX, CoinDCX, Zerodha-adjacent options) is like keeping valuables in a bank locker — convenient, but you're trusting the bank's solvency and security. Holding crypto in a SELF-CUSTODY WALLET (hardware wallet or seed-phrase-controlled software wallet) is like a home safe — full control, but full responsibility. Exchange collapses (FTX being the most infamous global example) have wiped out customer holdings that were technically "owned" by users but actually controlled by the exchange. For any meaningful holding size, moving off-exchange into self-custody is the standard risk-management practice — for a small learning-phase allocation, exchange custody is a reasonable trade-off for convenience.

Part 03The Indian Regulatory and Tax Picture — Read This Before Trading
RuleDetailPractical Impact
Flat 30% tax on gainsApplies to ALL crypto gains, regardless of holding period — no STCG/LTCG distinction like equityA 3-year-held Bitcoin gain is taxed exactly the same as a 3-day trade — no incentive structure for long-term holding, unlike equity
1% TDS (Tax Deducted at Source)Deducted on every sell transaction above a threshold, by the exchange, at the time of saleErodes capital on frequent trading — makes active crypto trading meaningfully more tax-costly than equity/options trading
No loss offsetCrypto losses CANNOT be set off against crypto gains, other capital gains, or any other incomeUnlike F&O (where losses offset business income) or equity (where losses offset other capital gains) — a crypto loss is simply gone for tax purposes, permanently
No deductionsCannot deduct transaction fees, internet costs, or other expenses against crypto incomeUnlike F&O trading, where brokerage/subscription costs are deductible business expenses

WHY THIS TAX STRUCTURE CHANGES THE STRATEGY: The no-loss-offset rule alone makes frequent crypto trading structurally worse, tax-wise, than equity or F&O trading of similar size and frequency — a string of small losing trades offers zero tax relief here, while the same losses in equity or options would reduce the overall tax bill. This is a genuine, quantifiable reason to treat crypto as a long-hold, low-frequency allocation rather than an active trading instrument, independent of any view on the assets themselves.

Part 04Where This Sits in the Plan

Crypto isn't part of the ₹60k/₹20k/₹15k/₹5k core capital allocation — it's foundation-layer knowledge, not a near-term deployment target. The regulatory and tax picture above is the more important takeaway than any price prediction: understanding that Indian crypto tax law actively discourages the frequent-trading style being built for equity and options is itself a useful piece of capital-allocation judgement.
